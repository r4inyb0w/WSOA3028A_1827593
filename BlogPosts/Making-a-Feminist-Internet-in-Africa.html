Response: Making a Feminist Internet (Magenya, 2020)

Friday, 19 June

“We need even more African feminists and feminisms on, in, around the internet.”

This article discusses the crucial need for African feminists to tell their stories and share their point of view to improve inclusivity and truth of experience on the internet. It is important to provide accurate information about the experiences of African women, those of the LGBTQIA community and people with disabilities. Additionally, there is a lack of resources on body-positivity and sexuality. This article makes a great point that producing this content, like fashion for fat women and make-up for boys, “is resistance made visible.” Magenya says “the same kind of kindness and care that we move in the world with when trying to understand the various problems that we live with, is needed online”.

Additionally, this article describes the internet as a “safe-space” for gender-based violence. Legislations that protect against theses attacks is “largely absent in many African countries”. As such, the reason African feminists on the internet is so necessary, is because we are in danger of the internet reflecting the unequal physical world we live in and this is due to unequal access and contribution to the internet. Magenya says the internet is “replicating existing oppression and suppression.” An example of discrimination against women over the internet, from mine and my friends' is experience, is in the online gaming community. It is a resource we turn to for entertainment and interaction, more so during lockdown, and we should be allowed to feel safe and enjoy ourselves. However, we often experience sexual harassment and sexist remarks from boys or men who feel safe to do so.

Furthermore, innovators in Africa resort to designing technology to improve lives in Africa, for example, to save people from drought and disease. It is not innovation for fun. So, Africans use their resources and skills to largely fix the problems caused by the Western world taking advantage of the continent, its people and resources. One such example is the pharmaceutical company, Pfizer, illegally testing anti-meningitis drugs on children. It is clear that Africans are restricted in their innovation opportunities because they are using their access to technology to improve lives.

This relates to one of my previous blog posts, A close reading: Towards a study of information geographies, which discusses the lack of content about Africa being produced in Africa. In general, this is true, but this article further shows information about women, the LGBTQIA community and people with disabilities in Africa, is produced on an even lesser scale by the right people.

Therefore, the push to get this fair content on the internet, by projects such as "All Women Count - Take Back The Tech!" is prevalent to start equalising the content and contributors on the internet, especially in the time of Covid-19 when people are turning to the internet to communicate and have company.

Mangenya concludes with this, which I believe sums up this issue perfectly and I couldn’t put it better myself: “Without a feminist internet that ensures that all people can access and influence content and governance of this internet, we will be replicating a violent and oppressive way of life that might eventually be inescapable.”

References
